Weekly Research Topics

Q(1) Less obvious perks of using 'live' data resources include logins and payments. How could Twitter be used in these two scenarios?

A(1) I think that using twitter as a login method would make it possible to create an account on multiple sites or services without the need for filling out the same information several times. With the payment however, I am not sure how exactly the security issues would be addressed, maybe Google Wallet or Paypal would be better suited for this area.

Q(2) Visit Programmableweb.com and search for JSON-P data sources. Why would we need JSON-P, specifically?

A(2) As developers we need to use JSON-P, for it provides a method to request data from outside websites/domains, this not allowed by typical web browsers, due to the same origin policy. Without JSON-P this task would be much harder to accomplish.

Q(3) Based on the JSON-P search results: what data resource(s) do you think might pair well with which types of Native device features?

A(3) Geolocation: Google Maps, Map Quest, Apple Maps, AccuWeather, Twitter, Facebook etc Camera/Capture: Instagram, Facebook, Flicker, Twitter Storage/sync: Dropbox, Copy, Google drive, skydrive, iCloud, etc

Q(4) Please describe any design and functionality intentions you may have for your Demo app components this month.

A(4) Since hunting season is right around the corner, I plan on making some kind of app to keep track of your hunting spots(not exactly sure yet, but it will be hunting related). For the camera I will be using Instagram to share photos of your kill with friends. For geolocation I will use Google Maps to store your hunting location.The rest will be determined as I go.

Q(5) Briefly describe some past 'lessons learned' regarding workflow as it relates to research, design, development, etc

A(5) With workflow I try to start early enough to work out issues which I might have, I try to use only the most trusted resources when researching a topic for the project.Withe the development portion, I like to take a few hours a day to work on the project, and then take a bit of a break to cool down, rethink some issues. Design is definitely my weakness, I suppose I don't have the design type of mind, I do try and pull ideas from other apps and combine them.

Weekly Research Topics

Q(1) What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with jQuery, for instance)

A(1) Honestly I think that currently the only thing which I could do to make the app more accessible would be using more section tags. However with al things I am certain that there are many more ways to improve upon the accessibility aspect of the html markup, which I am not yet familiar with.

Q(2) Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

A(2) Some common issues that I saw with Screen readers is the level of complexity, the users must first learn how to use these pieces of software, as well as have some basic knowledge of html. One last issue is the robotic nature of the experience which I feel puts people off from wanting to use these programs. I can see that the easiest way to relief these issues would be to make the web content more simplified. Another way could be the humanization of the programs used by the impaired population, however I am not quite sure how that would be accomplished, for I am not a specialist in that area.

Q(3) Devices: Please list two devices (and some of their features) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

A(3) iOS: VoiceOver Siri Speak Selection Dictation Zoom Large Text Invert Colors Mono Audio Visible and Vibrating Alerts Android: Speak passwords (speak passwords as you type them) Manage call answering/ending (use a physical button to end calls) Screen timeout (how long before the screen goes blank) TalkBack (speak all events that happen) Explore by touch (hear or see descriptions of what you are touching on the device) Negative colors (reverse colors of the screen) Change font size Mono audio (so both channels of audio are routed to single earpiece) Turn off all sounds Tap and hold delay

Q(4) Mobile Apps: Please list two apps (either Android or iOS, or both) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

A(4) iOS: Siri, Voiceover Android: Handcent SMS (hearing impaired), Google Voice (hearing impaired)
